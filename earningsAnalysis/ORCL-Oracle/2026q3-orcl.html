10-Minute Investor Briefing — Transcript

00:00 — Opening
Welcome & What This Quarter Means

Welcome to this retail investor briefing on Oracle's fiscal third quarter 2026 results, released today — March 10, 2026. If you own Oracle stock, are thinking about buying it, or simply want to understand what's happening in the cloud and AI infrastructure space, this transcript is for you.

The short version: Oracle had an exceptional quarter. Revenue and earnings both beat analyst expectations, cloud growth actually accelerated compared to last quarter, and management raised its long-term revenue guidance. The stock responded by jumping roughly 8.5% in after-hours trading. Let's break down why.

01:00 — Top Line Results
Revenue & Earnings — Both Beat Expectations

Oracle reported total quarterly revenue of $17.2 billion, up 22% from the same quarter last year. Wall Street had expected around $16.9 billion, so Oracle came in roughly $300 million above expectations — a solid beat.

On earnings per share, Oracle reported non-GAAP EPS of $1.79, beating the analyst consensus of $1.70. On a GAAP basis — which includes all accounting charges — EPS was $1.27, also up 24% year-over-year.

What makes this quarter historically significant is that it marks the first time in over 15 years where both total revenue growth and earnings-per-share growth simultaneously came in at 20% or more. That's a meaningful milestone — it signals the company isn't just growing fast at the expense of profits. Both sides of the ledger are accelerating together.

"Q3 fiscal 2026 was an exceptional quarter with financial results that exceeded expectations. This Q3 was the first quarter in over 15 years where organic total revenue and non-GAAP earnings per share both grew at 20% or more in USD."

— Oracle Press Release, March 10, 2026
02:30 — Cloud Breakdown
Cloud Is Now Majority of Oracle's Business

The most important driver of Oracle's growth is its cloud business, and this quarter cloud revenue hit $8.9 billion — that's 44% growth year-over-year and now represents 52% of Oracle's total revenue. For the first time, cloud is officially more than half of what Oracle sells.

Within cloud, there are two main segments. The first is Software as a Service (SaaS) — these are cloud applications like Oracle's Fusion ERP, NetSuite, and HR software. SaaS brought in roughly $4 billion this quarter, growing around 10% year-over-year. Solid, but not the exciting part.

The exciting part is Infrastructure as a Service, or IaaS — Oracle Cloud Infrastructure, known as OCI. This is the business where Oracle rents computing power, particularly Nvidia GPUs, to AI companies and enterprises. IaaS revenue hit $4.9 billion, growing an astonishing 84% year-over-year. Even more importantly, that's an acceleration from the 68% growth rate Oracle posted last quarter. Wall Street was worried growth might be peaking. Instead, it sped up.

Why does IaaS matter so much? Every major AI company — including OpenAI, xAI (Elon Musk's AI startup), Meta, Nvidia, and AMD — needs enormous computing power to train and run AI models. Oracle's data centers are packed with Nvidia GPUs, and these companies are paying Oracle billions to use them. AI infrastructure revenue alone grew 243% year-over-year this quarter.

04:00 — The RPO Story
The $553 Billion Backlog — Oracle's Biggest Asset

If there's one number that tells the story of Oracle's future, it's the Remaining Performance Obligations, or RPO. Think of this as Oracle's order backlog — contracts already signed but not yet billed as revenue. Oracle's RPO ended the quarter at $553 billion.

To put that in perspective: $553 billion is more than 8 years' worth of Oracle's current annual revenue. It grew 325% year-over-year — up from $130 billion just one year ago. It also grew $29 billion just since last quarter.

What's driving this enormous backlog? Mostly large-scale AI contracts. Oracle has signed massive multi-year deals to provide cloud infrastructure to the biggest names in AI. The company also made a specific point to reassure investors: most of these contracts don't require Oracle to raise additional capital, because customers either prepay for the computing capacity, or they supply their own GPUs and ask Oracle to run the data centers.

Multi-cloud database revenue also had a standout quarter. Oracle's database software — which now runs inside Amazon AWS, Microsoft Azure, and Google Cloud data centers — grew 531% year-over-year, according to Co-CEO Clay Magouyrk on the earnings call. Oracle is now live in 33 Microsoft regions, 14 Google regions, and expanded from just 2 to 8 Amazon regions during this quarter alone.

05:30 — Notable Deals & Partnerships
Key Customer Wins & Partnerships This Quarter

Oracle highlighted a number of major customer wins and partnerships during the quarter. On the enterprise side, new or expanding deals include Air France-KLM, Lockheed Martin, SoftBank, and Microsoft's Activision Blizzard video game division migrating workloads to Oracle Cloud.

On the AI infrastructure side, the Abilene, Texas data center project — a massive facility Oracle is building for OpenAI — saw two buildings come fully operational this quarter, with the rest of the campus on track. Oracle pushed back firmly against media reports that it had dropped plans to expand the site, calling those reports "incorrect." The OpenAI relationship remains one of Oracle's most strategically important contracts.

Oracle also confirmed it has secured a 15% stake in TikTok US as part of its strategic investments, adding another high-profile name to its ecosystem.

Larry Ellison, Oracle's founder and CTO, spoke on the call about the company's ambitions in AI-driven software:

"Thank God we have these coding tools now that allow us to build a comprehensive set of software, agent-based software, to implement, to automate a complete ecosystem like healthcare or financial services. That's what we're doing at Oracle. That's why we think we're a disruptor."

— Larry Ellison, Oracle Co-Founder & CTO, Q3 FY2026 Earnings Call
07:00 — Guidance & Outlook
Oracle Raises Its Long-Term Targets

Oracle provided strong forward guidance. For next quarter — Q4 FY2026 — Oracle expects total revenue to grow between 19–21% in USD terms, and cloud revenue to grow between 44–48%. Non-GAAP EPS for Q4 is projected at $1.99.

For the full fiscal year 2026, Oracle reaffirmed its revenue target of $67 billion and confirmed it expects capital expenditures of $50 billion — a massive investment in data center expansion. To fund this, Oracle raised $30 billion this quarter through investment-grade bonds and other securities, and the offering was reportedly oversubscribed (meaning investors wanted more than Oracle was selling).

Most notably, Oracle raised its fiscal year 2027 revenue guidance to $90 billion, up from the previous $89 billion target. Wall Street had only expected around $86.6 billion, so this guidance raise significantly exceeded consensus. If Oracle hits $90 billion in FY2027, that would represent roughly a 34% increase from FY2026's $67 billion target.

08:30 — The Stock Picture
Context: Oracle's Battered Stock vs. Its Growing Business

Oracle's stock closed today at around $151, down roughly 23% year-to-date and off more than 50% from its September 2025 peak of $344. That decline has largely been driven by investor concerns about Oracle's heavy debt load from building data centers, free cash flow that has turned negative (Oracle spent $13 billion more in capital than it generated in the last 12 months), and fears that low-margin GPU rental contracts are diluting the profitability Oracle used to enjoy from high-margin software licenses.

Tonight's results didn't erase those concerns entirely — restructuring charges doubled year-over-year to $153 million, and the legacy software license business continues to fade. But the market's after-hours reaction of +8.5% suggests investors are choosing to focus on the acceleration in cloud, the beat on both headline metrics, and the raised guidance.

For context: the average analyst price target on Oracle is around $253, implying significant upside from today's levels if the cloud growth story remains intact.

09:30 — Summary & Key Takeaways
What Retail Investors Should Remember

Let's close with the four things worth keeping in mind as a retail investor after tonight's results.

First, Oracle's cloud and AI infrastructure business is genuinely accelerating — not slowing down. The 84% IaaS growth rate this quarter was faster than last quarter, and the company's backlog of $553 billion gives it strong revenue visibility for years.

Second, Oracle is spending enormous amounts of money to build out data centers — $50 billion in capex for this fiscal year alone. This is a bet on AI infrastructure demand being real and durable. It works if AI demand keeps growing; it's a problem if demand softens.

Third, the multi-cloud database strategy is a genuine competitive differentiator. Running Oracle's database inside AWS, Azure, and Google Cloud — and growing 531% in that segment — shows Oracle is no longer siloed in its own cloud. It's threading itself into every major cloud.

Fourth, the stock is still down over 50% from its peak. The business is growing fast, but the question of whether free cash flow can return to strongly positive territory — and when — remains open. Tonight's results don't fully answer that, but they do show momentum in the right direction.
